\begin{abstract}
Industrial anomaly detection is commonly reported on static image sets, yet
inspection systems receive samples over time and must balance accuracy,
calibration, latency, and behavior under changing stream conditions. We present
ZIAD, a streaming evaluation protocol for zero-shot industrial anomaly detection
with CLIP-style baselines. Rather than proposing a new detector, ZIAD turns
static industrial anomaly benchmarks into controlled i.i.d. and bursty streams
and evaluates methods along accuracy, calibration, latency, contamination, and
memory axes. We instantiate the protocol on two industrial datasets, MVTec AD
and VisA, with four representative baselines: PatchCore, WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP,
and RareCLIP. The current evidence is candidate-stage and pending final audit,
but already illustrates why static AUROC alone is insufficient: accuracy,
calibration, and latency can rank methods differently across datasets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Industrial anomaly detection is often evaluated on static test sets, but
practical inspection systems receive images over time. This mismatch is
especially important for zero-shot anomaly detection, where CLIP-style models
can be attractive because they reduce category-specific training, but where
deployment still depends on latency, score calibration, stream order, anomaly
prevalence, contamination, and memory behavior. A single offline AUROC number
does not expose these operational dimensions.

This paper positions ZIAD as an evaluation protocol and analysis framework, not
as a new model. The protocol asks whether common zero-shot or training-light
industrial anomaly detectors preserve their behavior when test samples arrive
as streams. It also records whether a method is fast enough for repeated
inspection use, whether its scores are calibrated, and whether i.i.d. versus
bursty anomaly arrivals change the resulting metrics.

The main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item a streaming zero-shot industrial anomaly detection protocol that converts
static category benchmarks into controlled i.i.d. and bursty streams;
\item a category-sharded execution design that makes long evaluations
restartable while preserving dataset and label integrity;
\item a common baseline interface and multi-metric comparison across MVTec AD
and VisA with PatchCore, WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP; and
\item an accuracy--latency--calibration analysis that exposes operational
trade-offs hidden by static AUROC-only reporting.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Industrial anomaly detection.}
Industrial anomaly detection benchmarks typically measure whether a method can
separate normal product images from defective ones on static category-level test
sets. MVTec AD~\cite{Bergmann2019MVTec} and VisA~\cite{Zou2022VisA} are central
benchmarks for this setting, and training-light methods such as
PatchCore~\cite{Roth2022PatchCore} have become common reference points. ZIAD keeps
these datasets and baselines, but changes the evaluation unit from an unordered
test set to an ordered stream.

\paragraph{Zero-shot anomaly detection with CLIP.}
CLIP-style anomaly detectors aim to reduce category-specific supervision by
using vision-language representations or prompt-driven scoring. WinCLIP,
AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP are representative baselines in this direction
\cite{Jeong2023WinCLIP,Zhou2024AnomalyCLIP,He2025RareCLIP}. This paper does not modify
their architectures; it evaluates how their outputs behave under streaming
conditions and common deployment constraints.

\paragraph{Streaming and online anomaly evaluation.}
Streaming evaluation emphasizes sample order, distribution shift, bursty event
arrival, contamination, and resume-safe execution. These concerns are related
to online anomaly detection and monitoring protocols
\cite{Chandola2009AnomalyDetection,Bifet2010MOA}, but they are less visible in static
industrial anomaly detection leaderboards. ZIAD makes the stream construction
explicit so that i.i.d. and bursty arrivals can be compared with the same
baselines and metrics.

\paragraph{Calibration and reliability.}
Anomaly scores are often used as thresholds or alarms, so ranking quality alone
is not sufficient. Calibration metrics such as ECE
\cite{Guo2017Calibration} help quantify whether scores can be interpreted
reliably across streams, categories, and contamination settings. ZIAD therefore
reports ECE alongside AUROC, AUPR, latency, and CRD-lite rather than treating
AUROC as the only target.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\subsection{Stream construction}
The protocol converts a static anomaly-detection test set into stream-ordered
evaluation items with a common schema:
\texttt{stream\_index}, \texttt{image\_path}, \texttt{label},
\texttt{category}, \texttt{source\_split}, and \texttt{anomaly\_type}.
Stream generation preserves label integrity and avoids duplicate image paths.
When requested stream prevalence, contamination, or burst layout cannot be
matched exactly, the generator records the applied statistics and emits a
warning rather than silently fabricating a perfect stream.

\subsection{Baseline interface}
Each baseline consumes the same stream file and writes the same score schema:
\texttt{stream\_index}, \texttt{image\_path}, \texttt{label},
\texttt{category}, \texttt{anomaly\_score}, \texttt{latency\_ms},
\texttt{peak\_vram\_mb}, and \texttt{status}. This interface keeps the
protocol independent of a particular detector implementation. PatchCore and
RareCLIP use their default/SCS memory setting in the main candidate comparison;
WinCLIP and AnomalyCLIP use default/no-memory settings.

\subsection{Metrics}
ZIAD evaluates each detector along five axes. First, it compares i.i.d. streams
with bursty streams, where anomalous samples are concentrated into contiguous
blocks. Second, it evaluates contamination levels, including the current compact
candidate settings \(\epsilon \in \{0, 0.05\}\). Third, it records score
calibration, with the current main candidate comparison using no post-hoc
calibration. Fourth, it distinguishes no-memory baselines from methods with
memory or coreset-style state. Fifth, it reports latency alongside image AUROC,
AUPR, ECE, and CRD-lite.

\subsection{Category-sharded execution}
The candidate protocol currently covers MVTec AD and VisA, the baselines
PatchCore, WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP, stream length 64, and seeds
\(\{0,1,2\}\). Execution is category-sharded so long-running steps can resume
without confusing a completed category shard with a full paper result.
Promotion is deliberately separated from execution: candidate artifacts remain
pending final audit until row counts, category counts, metric validity,
baseline provenance, sampler settings, runtime semantics, and manual review are
complete.

\section{Experimental Setup}
The current candidate comparison is category-sharded. Each category shard
contains 12 metric rows: two stream types, two contamination settings, and
three seeds. MVTec AD contributes 15 categories and 180 rows per baseline.
VisA contributes 12 categories and 144 rows per baseline. All candidate outputs
are written under \texttt{results/latest/paper\_candidate/}, separate from
smoke and full-P0 production-validation outputs.

PatchCore uses the documented candidate sampler setting
\texttt{sampler\_percentage=0.1}. Baseline repository URLs and commit hashes are
pinned in the repository configuration; this draft intentionally does not
invent or replace them.

Runtime metadata is being tracked separately in
\texttt{docs/runtime\_environment.md}. The current audit/build environment
records an Intel Core i5-14500 CPU, Ubuntu 22.04.5, Python 3.10.12, and a
PyTorch 1.13.1+cu116 stack, but the original device path, model-loading scope,
batching, and timing granularity still require manual confirmation before any
latency value is promoted from candidate evidence to a final paper claim.

\subsection{Reproducibility and result governance}
The repository keeps execution and claim promotion separate. Current result
artifacts are marked as candidate-stage outputs with closed paper and claim
gates: \texttt{paper\_allowed=false}, \texttt{claim\_allowed=false}, and
\texttt{review\_status=review\_pending}. These internal flags are not part of
the scientific claim; they are engineering safeguards to prevent draft tables
from being mistaken for final reviewed results. Promotion to final claims
requires row-count and category-count checks, finite metric audits, provenance
review, runtime documentation, and manual approval.

\section{Discussion}
The current candidate results illustrate why ZIAD treats the benchmark as a
multi-objective evaluation. A detector that is strong in AUROC may not be the
best calibrated or fastest method on every dataset. The protocol therefore
separates image discrimination, calibration, latency, stream robustness, and
memory behavior. This separation is the main contribution of the paper: the
benchmark is intended to make operational trade-offs visible for zero-shot
industrial anomaly detection.

The current draft has not yet elevated temperature-scaling or memory-policy
variants into the main candidate comparison. Those axes are implemented in the
protocol and validated elsewhere in the repository, but the safest main paper
story is the compact calibration-none comparison plus a clear plan for
calibration and memory ablations.

\section{Limitations}
The present draft has four important limitations. First, the main numerical
results are candidate evidence pending final audit, not final reviewed paper
claims. Second, the compact candidate setting uses stream length 64; longer
streams may be needed before stronger deployment claims are made. Third,
latency values depend on the local software and hardware path; they remain
local-runtime dependent until the final hardware documentation and timing
semantics are complete. Fourth, calibration and memory-policy variants are not
yet promoted into the main candidate comparison, so the paper should avoid
broad claims about temperature scaling or online memory until those slices are
reviewed.
