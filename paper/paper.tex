\begin{abstract}
Industrial anomaly detection is commonly reported on static image sets, yet
inspection systems receive samples over time and must balance accuracy,
calibration, latency, and behavior under changing stream conditions. We present
ZIAD, a streaming evaluation protocol for zero-shot and training-light
industrial anomaly detection. Rather than proposing a new detector, ZIAD turns
static industrial anomaly benchmarks into controlled i.i.d. and bursty streams
and evaluates methods along accuracy, calibration, latency, contamination, and
memory axes. We instantiate the protocol on two industrial datasets, MVTec AD
and VisA, with four representative baselines: PatchCore as a strong
training-light reference, and WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP as
CLIP-style baselines. The resulting comparison illustrates why static AUROC
alone is insufficient: accuracy, calibration, and latency can rank methods
differently across datasets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Industrial anomaly detection is often evaluated on static test sets, but
practical inspection systems receive images over time. This mismatch is
especially important for zero-shot and training-light anomaly detection, where
category-specific training may be reduced or avoided, but where deployment
still depends on latency, score calibration, stream order, anomaly prevalence,
contamination, and memory behavior. PatchCore provides a strong training-light
reference point, while WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP represent CLIP-style
zero-shot baselines. A single offline AUROC number does not expose these
operational dimensions.

This paper positions ZIAD as an evaluation protocol and analysis framework, not
as a new model. The protocol asks whether common zero-shot and training-light
industrial anomaly detectors preserve their behavior when test samples arrive
as streams. It also records whether a method is fast enough for repeated
inspection use, whether its scores are calibrated, and whether i.i.d. versus
bursty anomaly arrivals change the resulting metrics.

The main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item a streaming industrial anomaly detection protocol for zero-shot and
training-light detectors that converts static category benchmarks into
controlled i.i.d. and bursty streams;
\item a category-sharded execution design that makes long evaluations
restartable while preserving dataset and label integrity;
\item a common baseline interface and multi-metric comparison across MVTec AD
and VisA with PatchCore, WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP; and
\item an accuracy--latency--calibration analysis that exposes operational
trade-offs hidden by static AUROC-only reporting.
\end{itemize}

The paper reports a compact evaluation slice generated from the repository
artifacts and uses it to study protocol behavior rather than to assert a final
leaderboard.

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Industrial anomaly detection.}
Industrial anomaly detection benchmarks typically measure whether a method can
separate normal product images from defective ones on static category-level test
sets. MVTec AD~\cite{Bergmann2019MVTec} and VisA~\cite{Zou2022VisA} are central
benchmarks for this setting, and training-light methods such as
PatchCore~\cite{Roth2022PatchCore} have become common reference points. ZIAD keeps
these datasets and baselines, but changes the evaluation unit from an unordered
test set to an ordered stream.

\paragraph{Zero-shot anomaly detection and CLIP-style baselines.}
CLIP-style anomaly detectors aim to reduce category-specific supervision by
using vision-language representations or prompt-driven scoring. WinCLIP,
AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP are representative baselines in this direction
\cite{Jeong2023WinCLIP,Zhou2024AnomalyCLIP,He2025RareCLIP}. This paper does not modify
their architectures; it evaluates how their outputs behave under streaming
conditions and common deployment constraints. PatchCore is included separately
as a training-light reference baseline rather than treated as a CLIP-based
method.

\paragraph{Streaming and online anomaly evaluation.}
Streaming evaluation emphasizes sample order, distribution shift, bursty event
arrival, contamination, and resume-safe execution. These concerns are related
to online anomaly detection and monitoring protocols
\cite{Chandola2009AnomalyDetection,Bifet2010MOA}, but they are less visible in static
industrial anomaly detection leaderboards. ZIAD makes the stream construction
explicit so that i.i.d. and bursty arrivals can be compared with the same
baselines and metrics.

\paragraph{Calibration and reliability.}
Anomaly scores are often used as thresholds or alarms, so ranking quality alone
is not sufficient. Calibration metrics such as ECE
\cite{Guo2017Calibration} help quantify whether scores can be interpreted
reliably across streams, categories, and contamination settings. ZIAD therefore
reports ECE alongside AUROC, AUPR, latency, and CRD-lite rather than treating
AUROC as the only target.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\subsection{Stream construction}
The protocol converts a static anomaly-detection test set into stream-ordered
evaluation items with a common schema:
\texttt{stream\_index}, \texttt{image\_path}, \texttt{label},
\texttt{category}, \texttt{source\_split}, and \texttt{anomaly\_type}.
For a category \(c\), a generated stream is written as
\[
S_{c,\tau,\epsilon,r}=\{(x_t,y_t,a_t)\}_{t=1}^{T},
\]
where \(x_t\) is the image at stream index \(t\), \(y_t\in\{0,1\}\) is the
normal/anomaly label, \(a_t\) is the anomaly type, \(\tau\in\{\mathrm{iid},
\mathrm{bursty}\}\) is the stream type, \(\epsilon\) is the contamination
setting, and \(r\) is the random seed. The i.i.d. setting samples the requested
normal/anomaly mix subject to available images. The bursty setting uses the same
item schema but places anomalous samples into one or more contiguous blocks.
Stream generation preserves label integrity and avoids duplicate image paths.
When requested stream prevalence, contamination, or burst layout cannot be
matched exactly, the generator records the applied statistics and emits a
warning rather than silently fabricating a perfect stream.

\subsection{Baseline interface}
Each baseline consumes the same stream file and writes the same score schema:
\texttt{stream\_index}, \texttt{image\_path}, \texttt{label},
\texttt{category}, \texttt{anomaly\_score}, \texttt{latency\_ms},
\texttt{peak\_vram\_mb}, and \texttt{status}. This interface keeps the
protocol independent of a particular detector implementation. PatchCore is
treated as a training-light reference baseline, while WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and
RareCLIP are treated as CLIP-style baselines. PatchCore and RareCLIP use their
default/SCS memory setting in the main comparison; WinCLIP and
AnomalyCLIP use default/no-memory settings.

\subsection{Metrics}
For each detector \(f\), the wrapper emits an anomaly score
\(s_t=f(x_t)\) and a measured latency for each stream item. ZIAD evaluates the
resulting score sequence along five axes. First, it compares i.i.d. streams with
bursty streams, where anomalous samples are concentrated into contiguous
blocks. Second, it evaluates contamination levels, including the compact
settings \(\epsilon \in \{0, 0.05\}\). Third, it records score
calibration, with the current main comparison using no post-hoc
calibration. Fourth, it distinguishes no-memory baselines from methods with
memory or coreset-style state. Fifth, it reports latency alongside image AUROC,
AUPR, ECE, and CRD-lite.

Image AUROC and AUPR are computed from \(\{(y_t,s_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}\) for each
stream. ECE is computed as a diagnostic after min--max normalizing emitted
anomaly scores, because the wrapped baselines do not all output calibrated
probabilities. Aggregate tables report means over the completed category and
seed shards for each dataset/baseline slice.

CRD-lite is the repository's lightweight contamination robustness diagnostic.
For a fixed dataset, category, stream type, baseline, memory policy,
calibration, prevalence, and seed, the implementation compares a contaminated
run at \(\epsilon\) to the matching \(\epsilon=0\) run:
\[
\mathrm{CRD\text{-}lite}(\epsilon)=
\frac{1}{2}\left[
(\mathrm{AUROC}_{0}-\mathrm{AUROC}_{\epsilon})+
(\mathrm{AUPR}_{0}-\mathrm{AUPR}_{\epsilon})
\right].
\]
Positive values indicate a measured drop under contamination, zero indicates no
drop, and negative values indicate that the measured AUROC/AUPR improved in the
contaminated run. This is a signed proxy used for compact diagnostics, not a
full theoretical robustness metric.

\subsection{Category-sharded execution}
The compact evaluation slice covers MVTec AD and VisA, the baselines
PatchCore, WinCLIP, AnomalyCLIP, and RareCLIP, stream length 64, and seeds
\(\{0,1,2\}\). Execution is category-sharded so long-running steps can resume
without confusing a completed category shard with a full paper result.
Measurement generation is deliberately separated from claim approval: outputs
are reviewed through row-count, category-count, metric-validity, provenance,
sampler-setting, runtime, and manual checks before being used for stronger
claims.

\section{Experimental Setup}
The current compact comparison is category-sharded. Each category shard
contains 12 metric rows: two stream types, two contamination settings, and
three seeds. MVTec AD contributes 15 categories and 180 rows per baseline.
VisA contributes 12 categories and 144 rows per baseline. All generated outputs
are written under \path{results/latest/paper_candidate/}, separate from
smoke and full-P0 production-validation outputs.

PatchCore uses the documented sampler setting of 0.1. Baseline
repository URLs and commit hashes are pinned in the repository configuration;
this paper intentionally does not invent or replace them.

Runtime metadata is being tracked separately in the runtime environment
document. The current audit/build environment
records an Intel Core i5-14500 CPU, Ubuntu 22.04.5, Python 3.10.12, and a
PyTorch 1.13.1+cu116 stack. The reported latency columns are averages of
wrapper-emitted \texttt{latency\_ms} values; wrappers may use baseline-specific
single-image or batch-amortized timing, so latency should be interpreted as a
local-runtime comparison until device path, model-loading scope, batching, and
timing granularity are manually confirmed.

\subsection{Reproducibility and result governance}
The repository keeps measurement generation and claim approval separate.
A governance record tracks whether outputs have passed row-count and
category-count checks, finite metric audits, provenance review, runtime
documentation, and manual approval. These safeguards are engineering controls
for reproducibility; the paper-facing claims are limited to the reviewed
compact evaluation slice described above.

\section{Discussion}
The compact results illustrate why ZIAD treats the benchmark as a
multi-objective evaluation. A detector that is strong in AUROC may not be the
best calibrated or fastest method on every dataset. The protocol therefore
separates image discrimination, calibration, latency, stream robustness, and
memory behavior. This separation is the main contribution of the paper: the
benchmark is intended to make operational trade-offs visible for zero-shot and
training-light industrial anomaly detection.

The present study has not yet elevated temperature-scaling or memory-policy
variants into the main comparison. Those axes are implemented in the
protocol and validated elsewhere in the repository, but the safest main paper
story is the compact calibration-none comparison plus a clear plan for
calibration and memory ablations.

\section{Limitations}
The present study has four important limitations. First, it reports a compact
evaluation slice rather than an exhaustive benchmark. Second, the compact
setting uses stream length 64; longer streams may be needed before stronger
deployment claims are made. Third,
latency values depend on the local software and hardware path; they remain
local-runtime dependent until the final hardware documentation and timing
semantics are complete. Fourth, calibration and memory-policy variants are not
yet part of the main comparison, so the paper avoids broad claims about
temperature scaling or online memory until those slices are reviewed.

\subsection{Stream-length sanity check}
We include a tiny stream-length sensitivity sanity check for MVTec AD bottle
with WinCLIP at stream lengths 64, 128, and 256. These measured rows are
preliminary appendix evidence only; they do not support a broad stream-length
robustness claim without additional categories, additional baselines, and
manual review.

\subsection{Threats to validity}
\paragraph{Construct validity.}
ZIAD measures stream order, burstiness, contamination, calibration, latency, and
memory behavior through practical proxies. CRD-lite is intentionally a compact
signed diagnostic rather than a complete robustness theory, and ECE is computed
after score normalization because wrapped baselines do not share calibrated
probability outputs.

\paragraph{Internal validity.}
The protocol enforces no duplicate stream images, label integrity, row-count
checks, development-time result governance, and category-sharded resume
behavior. However, wrapper differences, local dependency versions, and
model-specific preprocessing can still affect measured scores and timing.

\paragraph{External validity.}
The main comparison covers MVTec AD and VisA with four representative
baselines, but it is not an exhaustive industrial deployment study. The tiny
stream-length sanity check is especially narrow: one dataset category and one
baseline, with no claim that the trend generalizes.

\paragraph{Runtime validity.}
Latency is local-runtime dependent. The current runtime documentation records
the audit environment, but final latency claims require confirming device path,
model-loading scope, batching behavior, and timing granularity.

\section{Conclusion}
ZIAD reframes industrial anomaly detection evaluation around ordered streams
instead of unordered test sets. The protocol makes stream construction,
contamination, calibration, latency, memory behavior, and restartable execution
explicit while preserving compatibility with established industrial datasets
and representative zero-shot or training-light baselines. The current
comparison across MVTec AD and VisA shows that accuracy, calibration, and
latency expose different operational views of the same detectors, motivating
multi-metric reporting rather than AUROC-only leaderboards. The study does not
claim a new detector, final state-of-the-art ranking, broad stream-length
robustness, or a completed calibration/memory-policy ablation; those remain
subject to additional reviewed slices. By making these axes explicit, ZIAD
offers a reproducible path for evaluating industrial anomaly detectors under
streaming conditions.
